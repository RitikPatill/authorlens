I remember exactly where I was when Curiosity landed on Mars — sitting on my apartment floor at 1am, watching the NASA livestream with my roommate, both of us in disbelief that we were seeing real-time telemetry from another planet. When the signal confirmed touchdown, we actually yelled. My neighbors probably hated us.

Space does something to me that nothing else quite does. It reframes everything. When you really sit with the fact that we've put rovers on Mars and probes past the edge of the solar system, the petty stuff of daily life gets very small very fast.

But I'll be honest: the commercialization of it makes me nervous. I love that SpaceX has driven down launch costs and reignited public interest. I hate that the narrative has shifted to billionaires escaping a planet they helped damage. "Mars as backup Earth" is not a vision. It's an exit strategy for the wealthy.

The science justifies the cost, to me, completely. Understanding our solar system, detecting potentially habitable worlds, watching the universe's history through James Webb — this is humanity doing what it does best. The colonization pitch? That I'm not sold on. Not yet. Maybe not ever, depending on who gets to go.
