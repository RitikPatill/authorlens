Climate change represents one of the most significant challenges facing humanity in the twenty-first century. It is driven primarily by the accumulation of greenhouse gases in the atmosphere, resulting from industrial activity, deforestation, and the combustion of fossil fuels. Scientific consensus indicates that global average temperatures have risen approximately 1.1 degrees Celsius above pre-industrial levels.

The consequences of this warming are wide-ranging and well-documented. Rising sea levels threaten coastal infrastructure and low-lying communities. Extreme weather events, including hurricanes, droughts, and wildfires, have increased in both frequency and intensity. Biodiversity loss and disruption to agricultural systems represent additional areas of concern.

It is worth noting that the window for effective intervention is narrowing. International agreements such as the Paris Accord have established emissions reduction targets, yet many nations are not on track to meet these commitments. Policy mechanisms including carbon pricing, renewable energy subsidies, and regulatory frameworks have been proposed as viable mitigation strategies.

In conclusion, addressing climate change requires coordinated action across governments, industries, and civil society. The economic costs of inaction are projected to far exceed those of proactive investment in clean energy and resilience infrastructure. A comprehensive and sustained policy response remains essential for limiting warming to manageable levels.
