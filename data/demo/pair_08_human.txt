I had my first panic attack in the produce aisle of a grocery store. I was twenty-six, otherwise healthy, in the middle of picking out apples, and suddenly convinced I was dying. Heart hammering, vision narrowing, the whole thing. I left the cart and sat on the floor outside until it passed.

For the next two years, I didn't tell anyone. I just organized my life around avoiding whatever might trigger it. I stopped driving highways. I turned down invitations. I got very good at making excuses. Looking back, I was getting smaller and smaller.

Therapy changed everything, and I say that knowing it sounds like a cliché. My therapist was the first person who helped me understand what was actually happening in my nervous system — not weakness, not drama, just a misfiring alarm system that we could retrain. That framing unlocked something for me.

I still have bad weeks. Anxiety doesn't dissolve, it sort of... gets managed. I've built a toolkit: sleep like my life depends on it, limit caffeine, get outside, notice the catastrophizing early. None of it is glamorous. What I want people to know is that it's possible to get significantly better. That sounds small but when you're in the produce aisle on the floor, it's the most important thing anyone could tell you.
