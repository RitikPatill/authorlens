I grew up in a coastal town, and watching the shoreline creep closer to my grandmother's porch every summer wasn't something I read about in a textbook — I lived it. The storm surges got worse. The fishing shrank. Old-timers kept shaking their heads, saying they'd never seen anything like it.

Honestly, I didn't fully connect the dots until I was in college and my environmental science professor showed us forty years of tide gauge data. It clicked, finally. That slow-motion catastrophe had a name: climate change, and we were already deep inside it.

What frustrates me most isn't the science — that's been settled for decades. It's the paralysis. Every year I see another report, another summit, another promise, and then... not much. My cousins back home can't get flood insurance anymore. The oyster beds my uncle relied on are acidifying. These aren't abstractions.

I don't know what the answer is, not completely. But I do know that pretending we have unlimited time is a luxury we can't afford. The longer we wait, the more expensive — and brutal — the reckoning. We need policies that actually bite, not glossy pledges recycled every four years.
