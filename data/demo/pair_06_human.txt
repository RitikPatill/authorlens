My uncle lost his job at the plant when I was fifteen. I watched a man who'd worked since he was eighteen suddenly have nowhere to be every morning. It wasn't just the money — it was the structure, the identity, the sense that he mattered. He struggled for years. Still does, honestly.

That's why when I hear people dismiss universal basic income as "paying people to do nothing," I think they're imagining the wrong person. They're imagining laziness. I'm imagining my uncle — and the millions of people who want to contribute but can't find a foothold.

UBI isn't a perfect idea. I've got questions: How do you fund it without gutting other programs? Does it actually reduce poverty or just shift it around? What happens to wages if employers know everyone has a floor? These aren't rhetorical — I genuinely don't know the answers.

But the pilot programs have been more encouraging than I expected. People didn't stop working. They started businesses. They went back to school. They took care of sick relatives. Turns out, when you remove the sheer terror of destitution, humans don't collapse into sloth — they do things. Maybe that's obvious, maybe it's naive. I think it's worth finding out at scale.
